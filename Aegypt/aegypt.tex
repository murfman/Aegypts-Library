\dropcap{T}{he} goal I set for this project is to list all of the books and authors mentioned throughout \textit{Ægypt}, or \textit{The Solitudes}, by chapter. Full bibliographies for both real and fictional works are at the end of the document. If a chapter is missing below, then it did not have any books or authors mentioned within it. Books or authors listed in bold are fictional works created by Crowley. The Authors Mentioned subsections list authors that were mentioned within a chapter but did not mention a particular book, well I tried to keep it that way, but I wasn't always sucessful.

In most cases I tried to cite a work that would have been appropriate for the time frame of the story in which it is mentioned. In some cases, I have not been able to find a suitable edition and so cite a modern edition instead. Those will eventually be cleaned up to reflect either a copy of the work from the 16th century, one up to 1978, or one available to Crowley while he was writing.\footnote{This little rule I decided on early on has caused me no end of grief and will be the death of me I'm sure.}

This is probably the fourth or fifth version\footnote{I've lost count of how many versions of this I have created at this stage, it has been dozens upon dozens.} of this that I've done. The previous versions were for my the enjoyment of myself and a few others. This version I've labored long and hard at to get it as good as possible, which it turns out is another trap that I set for myself. I also made use of {BiBTeX} using the excellent  \href{https://www.jabref.org/}{Jabref} application which I originally used in conjunction with \href{https://www.xm1math.net/texmaker/}{TexMaker} and now with \href{https://texstudio.org/}{TeXstudio}. \textit{(Earlier versions of this were entirely text or Markdown.)} 

I'm open to any ideas as to how this can be updated and expanded. For any corrections or comments the reader can email me directly at murfman@pm.me.

Honestly, the plot of the 2,000 line poem isn't much more complicated than that. Understanding it, however, with its constant allegories and metaphors and Latinizations of words makes the original Spanish\ldots tricky.

Góngora\index{De Góngora, Luis} was born in Cordoba, Spain in 1561 and was one of the leading figures of the Spanish Baroque\footnote{The Spanish Baroque period of literature was characterized by a progressive complexity and themes centered on the loss of confidence in the Neoplatonic ideals of the Renaissance. It featured disappointment in the failure of those ideals, an overarching pessimism with the world because of those failures, and a preoccupation with the passage of time. The most famous Spanish Baroque prose writer is Miguel de Cervantes, author of Don Quixote.}. Góngora\index{De Góngora, Luis} left a mark on not just the literature of his time, but literature (especially poetry) ever since that few others can compare to. His style was formalized with a name, \textit{gongorism}\index{Gongorism}, and he has been an inspiration to a varied bunch of artistic movements ever since; from ``Generation `27'', an attempt to revive the Spanish Baroque style on the 300th anniversary of Góngora's death, to 20th century French Symbolists. 

He wrote The Solitudes in 1613 circulating it anonymously before it was published under his name post-mortem.

\begin{quote}
The first modern edition of \textit{The Solitudes}\index{The Solitudes of Luis Góngora} was brought out in 1927, the tercentenary of Góngora's\index{De Góngora, Luis} death. In \textit{The Solitudes}, a poem of about two thousand lines written in the silva meter (seven- and eleven-syllable lines in free pattern), Góngora defies convention and takes as his subject matter the exact opposite of what was expected in Spanish letters in his time. Instead of using the highest level of poetic diction to praise the most heroic and noble of subjects, Góngora employs it to praise the beauties of nature and the virtues of the country and of natural life, while deprecating the artificiality and the vices of the court. The characters who speak with wisdom and live with integrity and nobility are the peasants, not the nobility.

It is a paradox of Góngora's\index{De Góngora, Luis} art that, while his focus is on the rural and the natural as the authentic and the desirable, he expresses this vision in poetry of the most complex syntax and of allusively and metaphorically dense qualities accessible only to the intellectually trained reader of poetry, and certainly not accessible to the peasants whom he extols. The Solitudes describes rustic life in the most ornate diction.\footnote{Berliner, Donna. ``The Solitudes by Luis de Góngora y Argote.'' 2022. https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/literature-and-writing/solitudes-luis-de-gongora-y-argote}
\end{quote}

In hindsight, it is easy to see Pierce reading a poem known for its complexity that lauds the peasant life and requires some education to understand. It also seems to stand as a direct representation of people trying to understand the Ægypt Tetralogy, a seemingly quiant story about a failed professor moving to the country that happens to hide complex Hermetic hermeneutics and cosmology based around a little known, at least in the United States, heretical Italian monk that thought all magick was performed within the infinity of the mind.

The edition of \textit{The Solitudes}\index{The Solitudes of Luis Góngora} that Pierce is likely reading, and the one that Crowley himself definitely was, is The Solitudes of Luis de Góngora translated by Gilbert F. Cunningham from The Johns Hopkins Press in 1968. Crowley quotes both the translated English (lines 94-96, 108-111, then 112-116) and original Spanish (113-114 and 119-121) in Vita - Chapter Three.

Those last three English lines that Crowley quotes make me wonder if this is where Pierce gets the idea that Spofford has a crook. Later, in D\ae{}monomania, Pierce is recalling his arrival in the Jambs to Spofford, mentioning that he saw Spofford come over the hill, crook in hand. Spofford resolutely denounces the idea, however, saying he's never owned a crook. This is perhaps another subtle reference to an overarching theme of Pierce's throughout the series, he inability to distinguish what he has read from what is reality.
